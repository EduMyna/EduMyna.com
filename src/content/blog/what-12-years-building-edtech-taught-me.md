I've spent twelve years building education programs. Started with a physical incubator in Kochi (2009), pivoted to a digital platform (2015), partnered with Facebook and Apple on university programs (2018), built an LMS that's now deployed at universities (2019), and most recently designed transformative learning experiences through The Second Design (2022-2024).

Every stage taught me something I wish I'd known earlier. Here's what actually matters if you're trying to build education that works.

## 1. Communities Don't Scale—Platforms Do

**The hard truth:** In 2012-2015, Startup Village was thriving. Hundreds of startups, massive programs, real outcomes. But it was entirely dependent on a handful of people showing up every day for every startup. When outcomes were good, it was because we got lucky with our attention. When they weren't, there was no system to fall back on.

**What I learned:** At scale, one person can't help a thousand people. Online platforms like AngelList figured out how to create value for thousands without a human in the loop for every interaction. We hadn't. We were running an artisanal service and calling it a platform.

**The shift:** By 2015, we made a hard call—wind down physical operations, build for digital-first. Create progression models, structured cohorts, submission-based progress. Build systems that work without heroic individuals.

**For builders:** If your education model requires you (or your team) to personally show up for every learner interaction, you don't have a scalable product. You have a consulting practice.

## 2. Skills Come Before Outcomes

**The mistake:** For years, we asked "how do we help students start startups?" We ran programs, provided funding, connected to investors. But most students didn't have the fundamental capability to build what they envisioned.

**The turning point:** June 2017, we took a cohort to Silicon Valley. Visited Google, Facebook, met mentors from top companies. I came back with clarity about the gap: the Valley wasn't producing great founders because of incubators. It was producing them because the education system was building people who could actually make things.

**What changed:** We stopped asking "how do we help students start startups?" and started asking "how do we help students become genuinely capable?" Switched to developer skills. Real projects. Code that ships.

**The results:** Students who went through skills-first programs had fundamentally different outcomes. They could execute. They could get jobs. They could build MVPs that actually worked. The startup outcomes followed naturally for those who wanted them.

**For builders:** Don't optimize for the impressive-sounding outcome (startups, jobs, certificates). Optimize for the actual capability students develop. Everything else follows.

## 3. Mission Alignment Isn't Optional—It's Operational

**The contrast:** In 2018, we ran two major partnerships simultaneously:

**Facebook VR Partnership:** Facebook wanted to build VR skills among Indian students. They funded everything. We built programs, delivered content, ran cohorts. But Facebook had no deep vision for what Indian students should do with VR. They didn't want content open-sourced. They didn't want deep university integration. We delivered something reasonable that we couldn't build on.

**Freshworks Partnership:** Freshworks wanted SaaS developers for their ecosystem. They wanted curriculum open-sourced. They wanted students who could actually build. They wanted long-term university integration (Sastra University). The alignment was real, and the programs worked.

**What I learned:** Mission alignment isn't a "nice to have" for feel-good partnership decks. It's an operational requirement. When the funder doesn't own the mission, things drift. Timelines slip. Quality standards diverge. The work becomes transactional.

**For builders:** Before taking funding or partnerships, ask: does this partner genuinely care about the same outcomes we do? If not, the operational friction will eat you alive.

## 4. University Integration > Standalone Programs

**The pattern:** We ran dozens of programs. Some as standalone courses (students sign up, take course, get certificate). Some as university-integrated (students take as credit electives, integrated into their degree).

**What worked:** University integration won every time. Students showed up. Completion rates were higher. Outcomes were better. Faculty became advocates. Programs sustained beyond our direct involvement.

**Why it worked:**
- **Incentive alignment:** Credit = real stakes for students
- **Built-in distribution:** Colleges bring students at scale
- **Institutional buy-in:** Faculty and admin become partners, not just customers
- **Sustainability:** Programs continue even when we're not actively selling

**Example:** Apple iOS School @ Manipal (2018)—credit-integrated elective. Students got real iOS skills, Apple got talent pipeline, university got modern curriculum. Still referenced years later.

**For builders:** If you're building B2C education for college students, you're fighting uphill. Build for university integration instead. It's harder to set up, but infinitely more sustainable.

## 5. Open-Source by Default Changes Incentives

**The conventional wisdom:** Keep your platform proprietary. It's your moat. Charge for access.

**What we did:** In 2020, we open-sourced the Pupilfirst LMS. Full platform. All features. Universities could self-host.

**What happened:** Instead of losing customers, we gained trust. Universities that would never pay for proprietary SaaS suddenly wanted to work with us. They could inspect the code, modify it, own their data. We became partners, not vendors.

**The business model shift:** Revenue didn't come from software licensing. It came from implementation, customization, content creation, and running programs on the platform. Higher-value work. Better margins. More aligned incentives.

**For builders:** If you're in education, consider what you're actually selling. If it's just access to software, you're competing on price. If it's expertise, transformation, and outcomes—open-source your tools and charge for the value.

## 6. Practice Beats Content Every Time

**The pattern:** Every failed program had great content. Slides were polished. Videos were professional. Resources were comprehensive. Students didn't transform.

**What worked:** Programs where students spent 80% of time doing, 20% consuming. Ship a project every week. Get feedback. Iterate. Repeat.

**RealWorldReady numbers:** When we shifted to practice-heavy sessions (through The Second Design), 63% of students rated sessions 10/10. Not because content was better. Because they were shipping real work and seeing transformation.

**Specific example:** "Shitty First Drafts" session—students don't watch a lecture on perfectionism. They ship three terrible versions of something in 30 minutes. They viscerally learn that iteration beats waiting for perfect.

**For builders:** Cut your content in half. Replace it with structured practice. Your completion rates will thank you.

## 7. Solve One Problem Deeply Before Solving Everything

**The mistake:** For years, we tried to serve everyone. Physical startups. Digital startups. Developers. Non-technical founders. Students. Professionals. Every cohort was different.

**The breakthrough:** January 2015—we made a single decision: "Student startups only." Then later: "Developer skills only."

Suddenly, everything became clearer. What curriculum to build. What partnerships to pursue. What success looked like. What to say no to.

**What I learned:** Specificity unlocks strategy. "Education for everyone" is not a strategy. "iOS development for engineering students through university partnerships" is.

**For builders:** If you're trying to serve multiple audiences with fundamentally different needs, you're building multiple businesses. Pick one. Go deep. Expand later.

## 8. Systems Thinking > Feature Building

**The evolution:** We started building features: submission tracking, feedback systems, cohort management, progression models. But the real breakthroughs came when we understood the systems behind education:

- **Incentive systems:** Why do students show up? (Credit > certificates)
- **Feedback loops:** How fast do students know if they're on track? (Weekly > end-of-term)
- **Progression models:** What unlocks next-level capability? (Practice > content consumption)
- **Stakeholder alignment:** Who needs to care for this to work? (Faculty + students + admin, not just students)

**The difference:** Features are "can students submit assignments?" Systems thinking is "what incentive structures ensure students complete assignments and learn from feedback?"

**For builders:** Before building the next feature, map the system. Who are the stakeholders? What are their incentives? What feedback loops matter? Build for the system, not just the user.

## 9. Policy Work Is Product Work

**The realization:** By 2019-2021, we were doing AICTE conversations, CBSE programs, national-level partnerships. I used to think this was separate from "product work."

**What I learned:** Policy is product at scale. If you want to reach millions of students, you're not optimizing conversion funnels—you're changing regulatory frameworks, credit transfer systems, teacher incentive structures.

**Example:** Getting university credit for our programs wasn't a sales problem. It was a policy problem. Once solved, distribution happened automatically.

**For builders:** If your vision is "education for millions," budget time for policy. It's not a distraction. It's the main event.

## 10. The Right Structure Unlocks the Right Partnerships

**The evolution:**
- Started as nonprofit (Startup Village)
- Spun off as private company (SV.CO)
- Built open-source platform (Pupilfirst)
- Now building Section 8 (EduMyna)

**What I learned:** Structure determines who partners with you and how they trust you.

- **Nonprofits:** Get government trust, but struggle with sustainability
- **Private companies:** Move fast, but universities distrust profit motives
- **Open-source:** Builds trust, but needs clear business model
- **Section 8:** Social mission + revenue generation + tax benefits

**For builders:** Don't just default to "startup = Pvt Ltd." Think about who you need to work with and what structure makes them trust you.

## What I'd Tell My 2009 Self

If I could go back to 2009 when Startup Village was just starting, here's what I'd say:

1. **Build platforms, not programs.** Programs are heroic. Platforms are scalable.
2. **Skills first, outcomes second.** Capability is the only real moat.
3. **Partner with universities from day one.** Standalone is a grind.
4. **Open-source your tools.** Charge for transformation, not access.
5. **Practice > Content.** Always.
6. **Pick one audience and go deep.** Specificity unlocks everything.
7. **Mission alignment is operational, not emotional.** Choose partners accordingly.
8. **Think systems, not features.** Incentives beat interfaces.
9. **Policy is product work.** Don't avoid it.
10. **Structure = strategy.** Pick the legal entity that unlocks your partnerships.

## What's Next

These twelve years weren't wasted, even when we pivoted or shut things down. Every "failure" was a lesson that now shapes EduMyna—the integration of everything I've learned about infrastructure, pedagogy, partnerships, and policy.

If you're building in education and want to talk through any of this, reach out. The space needs more people who are willing to learn in public and share what works.
